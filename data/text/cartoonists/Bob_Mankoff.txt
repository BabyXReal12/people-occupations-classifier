
Robert "Bob" Mankoff (born May 1, 1944<ref name=USAToday>Wilson, Craig. "Top drawer at The New Yorker Mankoff makes his imprint as cartoon editor," USA Today (2 October 1997): D, 1:2.</ref>) is an United States American cartoonist, editor, and author. He was the cartoon editor for The New Yorker magazine from 1997 to April 29, 2017.<ref>{{cite news last1=Cavna first1=Michael title=As Bob Mankoff steps down as New Yorker’s cartoon editor, here are his favorite works url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/comic-riffs/wp/2017/04/28/as-bob-mankoff-steps-down-as-new-yorkers-cartoon-editor-here-are-his-favorite-works/ accessdate=April 29, 2017 work=The Washington Post date=April 28, 2017}}</ref> Before he succeeded Lee Lorenz as cartoon editor, Mankoff was a cartoonist for The New Yorker for twenty years.

==Early life==
Mankoff grew up in Queens, New York, and attended Music and Art High School<ref name=transcript>Mankoff, Robert. [https://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/articles/A56757-2004Oct23.html "Comics: Meet the Artist," (transcript),] Washington Post (November 5, 2004).</ref> (graduating in 1962) and Syracuse University (graduating in 1966).<ref name=bio>[https://web.archive.org/web/20131206093430/http://www.newyorker.com/magazine/bios/robert_mankoff/search?contributorName=Robert%20Mankoff Mankoff bio,] New Yorker website. Accessed April 5, 2013.</ref>

==Career==
Mankoff submitted more than 500 cartoons to The New Yorker for over two years before he had his first one published<ref name=transcript /> in 1977.<ref>[http://www.condenaststore.com/-sp/No-Thursday-s-out-How-about-never-is-never-good-for-you-New-Yorker-Cartoon-Prints_i8534483_.htm "No, Thursdays out. How about never—is never good for you?" New Yorker cartoon by Robert Mankoff], Conde Nast Online Store. Accessed April 5, 2013.</ref> One of his cartoons (captioned "No, Thursdays out. How about never—is never good for you?") is one of The New Yorkers most widely reprinted cartoons.<ref name=bio /><ref name="What Makes Us Get It">{{cite web   url=https://www.npr.org/2014/03/24/293761256/new-yorker-cartoon-editor-explores-what-makes-us-get-it   title=New Yorker Cartoon Editor Explores What Makes Us Get It   publisher=NPR   work=Fresh Air  author=Gross, Terry   date=March 24, 2014   accessdate=June 23, 2018}}</ref>

In 1992, Mankoff founded the online Cartoon Bank,<ref name=Fleishman>Fleishman, Glenn. [https://www.nytimes.com/1998/10/29/technology/new-yorker-cartoons-to-go-on-line.html?smid=pl-share "New Yorker Cartoons to Go on Line,"] New York Times (October 29, 1998).</ref> a licensing platform for New Yorker cartoons and art, with more than 85,000 cartoons available for sale. Mankoff was hired as New Yorker cartoon editor in 1997;<ref name=Fleishman /> he credits his administration of the Cartoon Bank as being an important reason for why he was chosen to replace Lorenz.<ref name=transcript /> Tina Brown, The New Yorkers editor at the time, said of Mankoff, "Bob is not only a brilliant cartoonist himself, hes also an impassioned promoter, defender and curator of the art of cartooning. … Hes put himself out to nurture cartoonists."<ref name=USAToday />

Mankoff has stated that his all-time favorite New Yorker cartoonist is Jack Ziegler.<ref name=transcript /> He has also cited Shel Silverstein as an artist he would have liked as a contributing cartoonist.<ref name=transcript /> Under Mankoff, the magazine has brought in a new generation of cartoonists (including a number of female contributors); notable names include Pat Byrnes, J. C. Duffy, P. C. Vey, Farley Katz, Emily Flake, and  Julia Suits. Mankoff usually contributes a short article to each issue of The New Yorker, describing some aspect of the cartooning process or the methods used to select cartoons for the magazine.{{citation needed date=April 2013}}

Under Mankoffs stewardship, in April 2005 the magazine began using the last page of each issue for the subsequently very popular "The New Yorker Cartoon Caption contest Caption Contest" (prior that date, the Caption Contest had appeared as a back-page feature in the magazines annual "Cartoon Issue"). Mankoff himself contributes cartoons to Moment (magazine) Moment magazines own monthly cartoon caption contest.<ref>[http://www.momentmag.com/cartoon/ "Moment Cartoon Caption Contest,"] Moment website. Accessed May 2, 2013.</ref>

As a lecturer on the appreciation of humor, Mankoff is represented by the Leigh Bureau.<ref>[http://www.leighbureau.com/speaker.asp?id=427 Mankoff bio], Leigh Bureau website. Accessed April 5, 2013.</ref>

Mankoff is mentioned in MC Paul Barmans song "Cock Mobster" on the 2002 album Paullelujah!<ref>Mankoff, Robert. [http://www.newyorker.com/online/blogs/cartoonists/2010/08/caption-rap.html "Rhyme Scheme,"] The New Yorker online: The Cartoon Bureau (August 26, 2010).</ref>

In April 2017, Mankoff left The New Yorker and was named Humor and Cartoon Editor at Esquire (magazine) Esquire magazine on May 1, 2017.<ref>Cavna, Michael. [https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/comic-riffs/wp/2017/05/01/bob-mankoff-named-humor-editor-for-esquire-after-exiting-new-yorker-job/ "Bob Mankoff named humor editor for Esquire one day after exiting the New Yorker,"] Washington Post (May 1, 2017).</ref>

==Personal life ==
Mankoff and his wife Cory live in Briarcliff Manor, New York; they have one daughter together, Sarah. Cory also had a son who died in 2012. <ref name=bio /><ref name="Briarcliff">{{cite news  last=Minzesheimer  first=Bob  title=Bob Mankoff, New Yorker cartoon editor, at the Burns  url=http://www.lohud.com/story/life/2014/04/21/new-york-cartoon-editotr-bob-mankoff/7979717/  accessdate=January 19, 2015  newspaper=The Journal News  date=April 22, 2014}}</ref>

== Films ==
The 2015 documentary, Very Semi-Serious, presents a behind-the-scenes look at the cartoons of The New Yorker, and features glimpses into Mankoffs career, his role at The New Yorker, and his personal life.  The film was produced by Redora Films.

Mankoff has edited at least 14 collections of New Yorker cartoons, including The Complete Cartoons of the New Yorker (Black Dog & Leventhal, 2004), a compilation of every cartoon published since the magazine was founded; the hardcover book is a 656-page collection of the magazines best cartoons published during 80 years, plus a double CD set with all 68,647 cartoons published to that point.
