Warren Sattler (born September 7, 1934)<ref name="CBG">{{cite web last=Miller first= John Jackson  url=http://cbgxtra.com/knowledge-base/for-your-reference/comics-industry-birthdays  title=Comics Industry Birthdays  work= Comics Buyers Guide  date=June 10, 2005  accessdate= December 12, 2010  archiveurl=https://www.webcitation.org/5trAbNQWw   archivedate=October 29, 2010}}</ref> is an United States American artist and cartoonist, who contributed work to many popular publications from the early 1960s through the 1990s.

==Biography==
A lifelong Connecticut resident,<ref>{{cite web archiveurl= https://web.archive.org/web/20070927212433/www.chs.org/comics/creators.htm  archivedate= September 27, 2007 title= Connecticut Talent publisher= Connecticut Historical Society url=http://www.chs.org/comics/creators.htm}}</ref> Warren Sattler started cartooning at an early age and was first published in a newspaper at age 15.{{citation needed date=January 2011}} He got his education at the Wilcox Technical School in Meriden, Connecticut.{{citation needed date=January 2011}} He later taught at the Famous Artists School from 1957 to 1962.{{citation needed date=January 2011}} In the 1960s, he assisted on the comic strips Barnaby (comic strip) Barnaby and The Jackson Twins. Sattler also drew comics that appeared in Help! (magazine) Help magazine under the direction of Harvey Kurtzman. His own strip, Grubby, ran from 1964 to 1997.{{citation needed date=January 2011}}

In the 1970s, Sattler produced art for comic books, in particular Charlton Comics. Some titles he worked on include Billy the Kid (Charlton Comics) Billy the Kid, Fightin Army, Fightin Marines, Ghost Manor (comics) Ghost Manor, Ghostly Haunts, and Yang. Additionally, he worked as an illustrator for Cracked (magazine) Cracked magazine, National Lampoon (magazine) National Lampoon, and Playboy. Sattler created a second syndicated comic strip in 1980: Swamp Brats, a Sunday strip Sunday-only strip. He ghostwriter ghosted on such newspaper strips as Bringing Up Father, Comics for Kids and Gil Thorp during the 1980s and 1990s.
