
Louis Perry "Louie" Anderson (born March 24, 1953) is an American stand-up comedy stand-up comedian,<ref name="Playing The Heavy">{{cite news url=http://www.nydailynews.com/archives/entertainment/1999/03/21/1999-03-21_playing_the_heavy_-_for_laug.html  title=Playing The Heavy - For Laughs Louie Anderson Turns Childhood Abuse & Weight Into the Stuff of Standup  author=Roura, Phil  authorlink=  date=1999-03-21  work=Daily News  publisher=  pages=  language=  archiveurl=https://www.webcitation.org/5ZNfVFlpj?url=http://www.nydailynews.com/archives/entertainment/1999/03/21/1999-03-21_playing_the_heavy_-_for_laug.html  archivedate=2008-07-17  quote=  accessdate=2008-07-17  location=New York  deadurl=yes  df= }}</ref> actor and television host. Anderson created the cartoon series Life with Louie, has written four books including Hey Mom: Stories for My Mother, But You Can Read Them Too published in 2018. He was the initial host of the third revival of the game show Family Feud from 1999 to 2002.<ref>{{cite web url=http://thegrablegroup.com/speaker/louie-anderson/ title=Louie Anderson work=The Grable Group accessdate=24 March 2016}}</ref>

For his performance on the FX (TV channel) FX comedy television series Baskets (TV series) Baskets, Anderson received three consecutive Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Supporting Actor in a Comedy Series nominations and won once in September 2016.

==Early life==
Anderson was born and raised in Saint Paul, Minnesota, the son of Ora Zella (née Prouty) and Louis William Anderson.<ref>{{cite web url=https://variety.com/2016/tv/awards/baskets-emmy-nominee-louie-anderson-1201817642/ title=‘Baskets’ Emmy Nominee Louie Anderson on the Role of His Career first=Geoff last=Berkshire date=21 July 2016 website=Variety.com accessdate=10 November 2017}}</ref><ref>[https://www.familysearch.org/ark:/61903/1:1:VCJT-6Y3] {{dead link date=November 2017}}</ref> Anderson is the second youngest of 11 children in his family. In a 2016 interview on WTF with Marc Maron, Anderson revealed that his mother actually gave birth to 16 children, but five of them — the first baby and then two sets of twins — died in childbirth.<ref name="Episode 715: Louie Anderson">{{cite news  url=http://www.wtfpod.com/podcast/episode-715-louie-anderson  title=Episode 715: Louie Anderson date=2016-06-13 publisher=WTF with Marc Maron}}</ref>  Anderson has described his father as "abusive."<ref>{{cite news title=Interview with Stephen Colbert work=Late Show with Stephen Colbert publisher=CBS date=April 10, 2018}}</ref>

Anderson went to Johnson Senior High School (Saint Paul, Minnesota) Johnson Senior High in Saint Paul.<ref name="A Real-Life Family Feud, Talk Show Host Repairs Family Ties">{{cite news url=http://www.cbsnews.com/stories/2000/01/05/48hours/main146416.shtml?source=search_story  title=A Real-Life Family Feud, Talk Show Host Repairs Family Ties  date=2000-07-20  publisher=CBS News  archiveurl=https://www.webcitation.org/5ZNhFWkBU?url=http://www.cbsnews.com/stories/2000/01/05/48hours/main146416.shtml?source=search_story  archivedate=2008-07-17  deadurl=yes  df= }}</ref>

==Career==
On November 20, 1984, Anderson made his network debut as a stand-up comedy stand-up comedian on The Tonight Show.<ref>{{cite web url=http://www.dead-frog.com/comedians/comic/louie_anderson title=Louie Anderson work=Dead-Frog - A Comedy Blog accessdate=24 March 2016}}</ref>

In late 1985, Anderson was cast as Lou Appleton alongside Bronson Pinchot on the Television pilot pilot episode of Perfect Strangers (TV series) Perfect Strangers for American Broadcasting Company ABC (which was known in this early stage as The Greenhorn). When the show was picked up, Anderson was replaced by Mark Linn-Baker in the role of Appleton (whose first name was then changed from Lou to Larry) as the producers didnt think the chemistry between Anderson and Pinchot was quite right. The show ran for eight seasons on ABC.

In 1986, Anderson had a small role in the singing-telegram scene in Ferris Buellers Day Off.<ref>{{cite web url=https://www.imdb.com/name/nm0004706/ title=Louie Anderson website=IMDb access-date=2016-03-17}}</ref>

In 1987, Anderson appeared in a comedy special on Showtime (TV network) Showtime.<ref name="TV Review; Louie Anderson, Comedian">{{cite news url=https://query.nytimes.com/gst/fullpage.html?res=9B0DE3DF113AF934A2575BC0A961948260&n=Top%2FReference%2FTimes%20Topics%2FSubjects%2FT%2FTelevision  title=Louie Anderson, Comedian author=OConnor, John. J. newspaper=The New York Times date=August 17, 1987}}</ref>

In 1988, Anderson played a role in John Landis film Coming to America, which starred Eddie Murphy and Arsenio Hall;<ref name=NYT/> and starred in the camp comedy The Wrong Guys.

In 1989, Anderson guest-starred on the first episode of the MuppeTelevision segment of The Jim Henson Hour.

In 1995, Anderson created and produced a Saturday-morning animated series for Fox Broadcasting Company Fox called Life with Louie.<ref name="focus on funny">{{cite news last=Bubbenheim first=Aaron url=https://pittnews.com/article/32473/archives/comedians-visit-to-focus-on-funny/  title=Comedians visit to focus on funny  accessdate=May 3, 2018 newspaper=The Pitt News date=March 17, 2005}}</ref> The series was based on Andersons childhood with 10 siblings, a sweet-hearted mother and a loud, war-crazed father. It also detailed how he was picked on for his weight, and how he used comedy to deal with the teasing. The show was a 3-year hit on Fox, and won two Daytime Emmy Awards for Daytime Emmy Award for Outstanding Performer in an Animated Program Outstanding Performer in an Animated Program.<ref name="focus on funny" />

===Blackmailing incident===
In 1997, Anderson was blackmailed by a man named Richard John Gordon. Gordon demanded money from Anderson, threatening to reveal to Tabloid (newspaper format) tabloids that Anderson reportedly sexually propositioned him in a casino in 1993.<ref name=LVReviewJournal>{{cite news url=http://www.reviewjournal.com/lvrj_home/2006/Mar-17-Fri-2006/weekly/6310172.html  title=Neon - Laughter and Tears  author=Weatherford, Mike  newspaper=Las Vegas Review-Journal  date=2006-05-17  archiveurl=https://www.webcitation.org/5ZNer6T1v?url=http://www.reviewjournal.com/lvrj_home/2006/Mar-17-Fri-2006/weekly/6310172.html  archivedate=2008-07-17  deadurl=yes  df= }}</ref>

Between 1997 and 1998, Anderson paid Gordon $100,000 in hush money, fearing the story would threaten his starring roles in two family-oriented series, but when Gordons demands increased to $250,000 in 2000, Andersons lawyer informed federal authorities. Gordon, who was 31 at the time, was arrested after leading FBI agents on a high-speed chase along Santa Monica Boulevard.<ref name=LVReviewJournal/><ref>{{cite web url=http://www.eonline.com/news/article/index.jsp?uuid=a4fa14f7-0bf1-403a-b0cf-9fb60ee30a28  publisher=E!
