
 Drew Allison Carey<ref>{{Cite web url=https://twitter.com/DrewFromTV/status/743201079085539328 title=Drew Carey on Twitter website=Twitter access-date=2016-06-15}}</ref> (born May 23, 1958) is an American actor, comedian, sports executive, and game show host. After serving in the United States Marine Corps U.S. Marine Corps and making a name for himself in stand-up comedy, Carey eventually gained popularity starring in his own Situation comedy sitcom, The Drew Carey Show, and serving as host of the U.S. version of the improv comedy show Whose Line Is It Anyway? (U.S. TV series) Whose Line Is It Anyway?, both of which aired on American Broadcasting Company ABC.

Drew Carey has appeared in several films, television series, music videos, a made-for-television film, and a computer game. He has hosted the game show The Price Is Right (U.S. game show) The Price Is Right since 2007 on CBS. He is interested in a variety of sports and has worked as a photographer at United States mens national soccer team U.S. National Team soccer games. He is a minority owner of the Major League Soccer team Seattle Sounders FC. Carey has written an autobiography, Dirty Jokes and Beer Dirty Jokes and Beer: Stories of the Unrefined, detailing his early life and television career.

==Acting career==

===Early roles===
His early stand-up career led to supporting roles on television shows, during which he developed the character of a hapless middle-class bachelor. In 1993, Carey had a small role in the film Coneheads as a taxi passenger. In 1994, Carey co-starred with John Caponera in The Good Life (1994 TV series) The Good Life, a short-lived Situation comedy sitcom on NBC.<ref name="GoodLife">{{Cite news last=Bickelhaupt first=Susan title=Drew Careys Good Life work=The Boston Globe url=http://nl.newsbank.com/nl-search/we/Archives?p_product=BG&p_theme=bg&p_action=search&p_maxdocs=200&p_topdoc=1&p_text_direct-0=0EADE0BE65C28420&p_field_direct-0=document_id&p_perpage=10&p_sort=YMD_date:D&s_trackval=GooglePM registration=yes date=March 15, 1994 accessdate=July 16, 2011}}</ref> After the show was cancelled, Bruce Helford, a writer on the show, hired Carey as a consultant for the television show Someone Like Me.<ref name="SomeoneMe">{{Cite news  last=Johnson  first=Allan  title=Drew Carey Succeeds By Being Himself  work=Chicago Tribune  url=http://articles.chicagotribune.com/1997-11-16/news/9711160210_1_drew-carey-show-character-working  date=November 16, 1997  archiveurl=https://www.webcitation.org/60E5RNF28?url=http://articles.chicagotribune.com/1997-11-16/news/9711160210_1_drew-carey-show-character-working  archivedate=July 16, 2011  deadurl=yes  df= }}</ref>

===The Drew Carey Show===
After their stint on Someone Like Me, Carey and Helford developed and produced the storyline for The Drew Carey Show. The sitcom revolved around a fictionalized version of Carey, as he took on the stresses of life and work with his group of childhood friends. The show premiered on September 13, 1995, on American Broadcasting Company ABC. In his autobiography, Carey revealed his frustration with having to deal with censorship censors and being unable to employ the off-color humor common in his stand-up routines.<ref name="auto"/> Carey initially earned $60,000 per episode in the first seasons, then renegotiated for $300,000.<ref name="DCSSalary">{{Cite news  last=Huff  first=Richard  title=Drew to Carey on with Sitcom & Laugh All the Way to the Bank  work=Daily News (New York) Daily News  url=http://www.nydailynews.com/archives/entertainment/1998/02/27/1998-02-27_drew_to_carey_on_with_sitcom.html  date=February 27, 1998  archiveurl=https://www.webcitation.org/60E5VFktv?url=http://articles.nydailynews.com/1998-02-27/entertainment/18067265_1_drew-carey-show-wb-network-morning-america  archivedate=July 16, 2011  deadurl=yes  df= }}</ref> By the final season, he was earning $750,000 per episode.<ref name="DCSSalaryFinal">{{Cite news  author=Associated Press  title=Drew Carey going out with a whisper  work=USA Today  url=https://www.usatoday.com/life/television/news/2004-05-03-carey-going-off-air_x.htm  date=May 3, 2004  archiveurl=https://www.webcitation.org/60E5erF1f?url=http://www.usatoday.com/life/television/news/2004-05-03-carey-going-off-air_x.htm  archivedate=July 16, 2011  deadurl=yes  df= }}</ref> The show had high ratings for its first few seasons, but declining ratings and increasing production costs (around $3 million per episode) precipitated its cancellation.<ref name="DCSSalaryFinal"/><ref>{{cite news  last=Gillespie  first=Nick  author2=Steve Kurtz  title=Stand-Up Guy  work=Reason (magazine) Reason  url=http://reason.com/archives/1997/11/01/stand-up-guy  date=November 1997  archiveurl=https://www.webcitation.org/60Fw91hGn?url=http://reason.com/archives/1997/11/01/stand-up-guy  archivedate=July 17, 2011  deadurl=yes  df= }}</ref> The program had a total of 233 episodes over its nine-year run, and Carey was one of four actors to appear in every episode.

==Game show host==

===Power of 10===
Beginning in 2007, Carey began hosting game shows, beginning with his April selection as host of the CBS game show pilot Power of 10 (U.S. game show) Power of 10. The show ran from August 7, 2007 to January 23, 2008 and aired twice weekly during the late summer and early fall. Each game featured contestants predicting how a cross-section of Americans responded to questions covering a wide variety of topics in polls conducted by CBS.<ref name="Power10">{{Cite news last=Hale  first=Mike  title=Drew Careys "Power of 10" draws on his persona  work=The Denver Post  url=http://www.denverpost.com/ci_6633628?source=bb  date=August 17, 2007  archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20090710012107/http://www.denverpost.com/ci_6633628?source=bb  archivedate=July 10, 2009  deadurl=yes  df= }}</ref>
