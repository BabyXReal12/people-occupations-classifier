
Judd Apatow (born December 6, 1967) is an American producer, writer, director, actor and stand-up comedian. He is the founder of Apatow Productions, through which he produced and developed the television series Freaks and Geeks, Undeclared, Girls (TV series) Girls, Love (TV series) Love, and Crashing (U.S. TV series) Crashing and directed the films The 40-Year-Old Virgin (2005), Knocked Up (2007), Funny People (2009), This Is 40 (2012), and Trainwreck (film) Trainwreck (2015).
 
Apatows work has won numerous awards including a Primetime Emmy Award, a Hollywood Comedy Award, and an American Film Institute#AFI Awards AFI Award for Bridesmaids (2011 film) Bridesmaids (2011).<ref>{{cite web title=Behind the Scenes of THRs Judd Apatow Cover Shoot date=December 13, 2012 url=http://www.hollywoodreporter.com/video/scenes-thrs-judd-apatow-cover-401034 work=The Hollywood Reporter accessdate=July 1, 2013}}</ref><ref>{{cite web title=Deadline Hollywood date=October 23, 2012 url=http://www.deadline.com/2012/10/358432 work=Deadline Hollywood accessdate=November 11, 2012}}</ref> His films have also been nominated for Grammy Awards, PGA Awards, Golden Globe Awards, and Academy Awards.{{citation needed date=April 2018}}
 
His producing credits include Anchorman: The Legend of Ron Burgundy (2004),  Talladega Nights: The Ballad of Ricky Bobby (2006), Superbad (film) Superbad (2007), Forgetting Sarah Marshall (2008), Pineapple Express (film) Pineapple Express (2008), Get Him to the Greek (2010), Bridesmaids (2011 film) Bridesmaids (2011), The Five-Year Engagement (2012), Anchorman 2: The Legend Continues (2013), Begin Again (film) Begin Again (2014), Popstar: Never Stop Never Stopping (2016), and The Big Sick (2017).

==Career==

===1985–2003: Stand-up, early film and television work===
Apatow began performing stand-up comedy at age seventeen, during his senior year of high school.<ref name="nytimes"/> In the September 1985 issue of Laugh Factory Magazine, he is listed as an Associate Editor. After graduating from high school in 1985, he moved to Los Angeles and enrolled in the screenwriting program at University of Southern California.<ref name="nytimes"/><ref>{{cite web url=http://cinema.usc.edu/alumni/notable.cfm title=Sampling of Notable USC Alumni Working in Cinema/Television/Interactive publisher=USC School of Cinematic Arts accessdate=December 21, 2011}}</ref>  While at USC, he organized and hosted a number of on-campus "Comedy Night" events, featuring headliners such as Saturday Night Live performer Kevin Nealon. Apatow introduced the acts at these events with short standup routines of his own. He also began volunteering at (and later producing) benefit concerts for HBOs Comic Relief (charity) Comic Relief and performing and seeing standup at the Improv in Hollywood.<ref name="nytimes"/> He dropped out of college during his second year and later moved into an apartment with comedian Adam Sandler, whom he met at the Improv.<ref name="nytimes"/> He competed in the Johnnie Walker Comedy Search in 1989 directed by Saturday Night Live short film producer Neal Marshad.<ref name="latimes">{{cite news last=Abramowitz first=Rachel url=http://www.latimes.com/entertainment/news/la-ca-apatow13may13,1,1056915.story title=The king of Geeks work=Los Angeles Times date=May 13, 2007 accessdate=June 4, 2007}}</ref>

Shortly thereafter, Apatow was introduced by manager Jimmy Miller to Garry Shandling which resulted in Apatow being hired as a writer for the Grammy Awards that year, which Shandling hosted.<ref name="Daily Variety">{{cite news last=Newman first=Melinda title=Man most likely to inspire work=Daily Variety date=October 29, 2012 accessdate=November 23, 2012}}</ref> In 1992, Apatow appeared on Home Box Office HBOs 15th Annual Young Comedians Special<ref name="wild">{{cite news last=Michaels first=Chad title=Judd Apatow Interview publisher=Wild About Movies date=May 23, 2007 url=http://www.wildaboutmovies.com/interviews/Judd-Apatow-Interview.php accessdate=June 4, 2007}}</ref> and shortly afterwards went on to co-create and executive produce The Ben Stiller Show for Fox Broadcasting Company Fox. Apatow had met Stiller outside of an Elvis Costello concert in 1990, and they became friends. Despite critical acclaim and an Emmy Award for Apatow and the rest of the writing staff, Fox canceled the show in 1993.<ref name="Vanity Fair">{{cite news last=Marcus  first=Bennett url=http://www.vanityfair.com/online/daily/2012/11/judd-apatow-ben-stiller-show-comedy  title=
Judd Apatow and Ben Stiller on The Ben Stiller Show and Comedy—and How Cult Followings Mean the Most work=Vanity Fair (magazine) Vanity Fair date=November 12, 2012 accessdate=November 23, 2012}}</ref>

Apatow went on to join HBOs The Larry Sanders Show in 1993 as a writer and consulting producer, and he later served as a co-executive producer and director of an episode during the shows final season in 1998. He credits Shandling as his mentor for influencing him to write comedy that is more character-driven.<ref name="latimes"/> Apatow earned six Emmy nominations for his work on Larry Sanders. During this same time, he worked as a consulting producer and staff writer for the animated show The Critic, starring Jon Lovitz.<ref name="IFC">{{cite news last=Locker first=Melissa url=http://www.ifc.com/fix/2010/12/12-days-of-judd-apatow-day-5-t title=12 Days of Judd Apatow: Day 5 "The Critic" work=IFC date=December 21, 2010 accessdate=November 23, 2012}}</ref>

In 1995, Apatow co-wrote (with Steve Brill) the feature film Heavyweights. Around the same time, Apatow was hired to produce and do an uncredited re-write of the script for the movie The Cable Guy, which was released in 1996 to mixed reviews.<ref name="variety">{{cite news last=Thompson first=Anne url=http://www.variety.com/article/VR1117964732.html?categoryid=2508&cs=1 title=Hot Apatow new king of comedy work=Variety (magazine) Variety date=May 11, 2007 accessdate=June 4, 2007}}</ref> It was during the pre-production of the film that Apatow met his future wife, actress Leslie Mann.<ref name=actors/> Apatow did uncredited re-writes on two other Jim Carrey films: Liar, Liar and Bruce Almighty.<ref name="E! Online">{{cite news last=Haberman first=Lia url=http://www.eonline.com/news/45301/diaz-and-carrey-reunited title=Diaz and Carrey Reunited? publisher=E! date=June 11, 2003 accessdate=November 23, 2012}}</ref> His next script was titled Making Amends, which had Owen Wilson attached to star as a man in Alcoholics Anonymous who decides to apologize to everyone he has ever hurt. However, the film was never made.<ref name="variety"/> Apatow did uncredited rewrites of the Adam Sandler films Happy Gilmore and  The Wedding Singer.<ref name="variety"/> He was also featured in four tracks on Sandlers 1996 comedy album "What the Hell Happened to Me?"<ref>{{cite web author1=Eduardo Rivadavia (review) title=What the Hell Happened to Me? - Adam Sandler url=http://www.allmusic.com/album/what-the-hell-happened-to-me-mw0000179861 website=AllMusic accessdate=July 29, 2016}}</ref>

In 1999, Apatow created Sick in the Head, a multi-camera sitcom pilot starring David Krumholtz as a psychiatrist on his first day on the job, Amy Poehler as a suicidal patient, and Kevin Corrigan as Krumholtzs slacker roommate.<ref name="Splitsider">{{cite news last=Evans first=Bradford url=http://splitsider.com/2011/05/the-lost-projects-of-judd-apatow title=The Lost Projects of Judd Apatow work=Splitsider date=May 12, 2011 accessdate=November 23, 2012}}</ref> The show was not picked up by Fox, which freed up Apatow to serve as an executive producer of the award-winning series Freaks and Geeks, which debuted in 1999. He also wrote and directed several episodes of the series. After its cancellation, Apatow was the executive producer and creator of the series Undeclared, which reused Seth Rogen in the main cast and other Freaks and Geeks cast members in recurring roles. Although both shows were quickly canceled, USA Today media critic Susan Wloszczyna called the shows "two of the most acclaimed TV series to ever last only one season."<ref name="usatoday">{{cite news last=Wloszczyna first=Susan url=https://www.usatoday.com/life/movies/news/2007-05-06-judd-apatow_N.htm title=For Apatow, opportunity knocks work=USA Today accessdate=June 4, 2007 date=May 6, 2007}}</ref>

In 2001, Apatow created Julie, a pilot of his that featured Jason Segel, Kevin Hart (actor) Kevin Hart, Seth Rogen, Phil Hendrie, and Judge Reinhold (playing himself). The pilot was not picked up by Fox.{{citation needed date=April 2018}} In 2002, he co-wrote (with Brent Forrester) a Fox pilot titled Life on Parole, starring David Herman as a dissatisfied parole officer whose roommate happens to be one of his parolees; it was not picked up. Apatow has screened and introduced them at "The Other Network", a festival of un-aired TV pilots produced by Un-Cabaret.<ref name="Splitsider"/> He reunited with Jason Segel and Amy Poehler for the 2001 Fox sitcom pilot, North Hollywood.{{citation needed date=April 2018}}

===2004–2008: The 40-Year-Old Virgin, Knocked Up, and producing===
In 2004, Apatow film producer produced the feature film comedy Anchorman: The Legend of Ron Burgundy, starring Will Ferrell and directed by Adam McKay.<ref name=actors/> The film was a box office success. Apatow co-produced the 2013 sequel, Anchorman 2: The Legend Continues.<ref name="Los Angeles Times">{{cite news last=Day first=Patrick url=http://latimesblogs.latimes.com/showtracker/2012/03/anchorman-2-will-ferrell-ron-burgundy-conan.html title=Anchorman 2: Will Ferrell drops sequel news on Conan work=Los Angeles Times accessdate=November 25, 2012 date=March 29, 2012}}</ref> He made his feature directorial debut in 2005 with the comedy The 40-Year-Old Virgin, which he also co-wrote with the films star, Steve Carell, for Universal Pictures. The film opened at number one at the box office and grossed more than $175 million globally.<ref name="variety"/> The comedy garnered numerous awards and nominations, including being named one of American Film Institute AFIs Top Movies of the Year, as well as taking home Best Comedy Movie at the 11th annual Critics Choice Awards.<ref>{{cite web title=AFI Announces Their Selection of 2005s Best Movies publisher=About.com date=August 18, 2006 url=http://movies.about.com/od/awards/a/afi12005121105.htm accessdate=November 25, 2012}}</ref> The 40-Year-Old Virgin also earned Apatow a nomination for Best Original Screenplay from the Writers Guild of America and received four MTV Movie Award nominations, including a win for Carell for Best Comedic Performance.<ref>{{cite news last=Buckalew first=Brett title=No Longer a Feature Film Virgin publisher=Filmstew.com date=August 18, 2006 url=http://www.filmstew.com/showArticle.aspx?ContentID=14646 accessdate=June 4, 2007}}</ref>

His second film, the romantic comedy Knocked Up, was released in June 2007 to wide critical acclaim. Apatow wrote the initial draft of the film on the set of Talladega Nights: The Ballad of Ricky Bobby Talladega Nights.<ref name="latimes"/> The story concerns a slacker and a media personality (Rogen and Heigl, respectively) whose one-night stand results in an  unintended pregnancy. In addition to being a critical success, the film was also a commercial hit, continuing Apatows newfound mainstream success.

File:Judd Apatow.jpg thumbnail Apatow in 2007
In August 2007, Apatow produced the film Superbad (film) Superbad, which was written by Seth Rogen and his writing partner Evan Goldberg. A concept Rogen and Goldberg had created as teens, Apatow convinced Rogen to write the film as a vehicle for himself in 2000. Rogen and Goldberg finished writing the film, but were unable to find a studio interested in producing it. Apatow then enlisted Rogen and Goldberg to write Pineapple Express (film) Pineapple Express, a Drug culture stoner action movie that he felt would be more commercial. After the success of Anchorman and The 40-Year-Old Virgin, Apatow was still unable to sell both Superbad and Pineapple Express; it was only after he produced the commercial hit Talladega Nights that Sony Pictures Entertainment decided to produce both.<ref name="variety"/>

At this point, Rogen was unable to play the lead for Superbad, as he had grown too old to play the part of Seth. Subsequently, he was cast in a supporting role as a police officer and friend Jonah Hill took his role as the high school student. Apatow credits Rogen for influencing him to make his work more "outrageously dirty."<ref name="latimes"/>

In August 2007, Superbad opened at No. 1 in the box office to critical acclaim, taking in $33 million in its opening weekend.<ref>{{cite news  last=Coyle  first=Jake  title=How Did Superbad Top the Box Office?  work=NewsOK  agency=Associated Press date=August 21, 2007  url=http://newsok.com/article/3107893}}</ref> Industry insiders claimed Apatow was now a brand unto himself, creating movies geared toward older audiences, who would watch his movies even when the films delved into the teen genre.<ref>{{cite news last=Dergarabedian first=Paul title=Paul D. Explains the Judd Apatow Phenomenon work=New York (magazine) New York publisher=New York Media date=August 20, 2007 url=http://nymag.com/daily/entertainment/2007/08/paul_dergarabedian_watch_paul_d.html accessdate=August 26, 2007}}</ref>

Apatow served as producer and co-writer along with director Jake Kasdan for the biopic spoof Walk Hard: The Dewey Cox Story Walk Hard starring John C. Reilly, Kristen Wiig and Jenna Fischer, which was released in December 2007.<ref>{{cite web last1=Dargis first1=Manohla title=Behind the Music, This Time for Laughs url=https://www.nytimes.com/2007/12/21/movies/21walk.html website=The New York Times accessdate=July 29, 2016 date=December 21, 2007}}</ref> While the film received positive reviews,<ref>{{cite web title=Walk Hard: The Dewey Cox Story (2007) url=https://www.rottentomatoes.com/m/walk_hard/ publisher=Rotten Tomatoes accessdate=July 29, 2016}}</ref> it did poorly commercially.<ref>{{cite web title=Walk Hard: The Dewey Cox Story url=http://www.boxofficemojo.com/movies/?id=walkhard.htm publisher=Box Office Mojo accessdate=July 29, 2016}}</ref> In 2008, he served as producer for Drillbit Taylor starring Owen Wilson and Leslie Mann and written by Seth Rogen, which opened in March and earned negative reviews. Apatow retracted his support for the movie, feeling it was not what he had initially envisioned.<ref>{{cite web last1=Brevet first1=Brad title=Judd Apatow Disowns ‘Drillbit Taylor’ url=http://www.comingsoon.net/movies/news/525670-judd_apatow_disowns_drillbit_taylor website=ComingSoon.net accessdate=July 29, 2016 date=March 20, 2008}}</ref>

For the rest of 2008, Apatow produced the comedy films Forgetting Sarah Marshall starring Jason Segel and Kristen Bell; Step Brothers (film) Step Brothers, which reunites Talladega Nights co-stars Will Ferrell and John C. Reilly; and Pineapple Express (film) Pineapple Express starring Seth Rogen and James Franco, both of whom originally starred on Freaks and Geeks. In addition, he served as co-writer for the Adam Sandler comedy film You Dont Mess with the Zohan, which Sandler and Robert Smigel also co-wrote.<ref>{{cite web last1=Leitch first1=Will last2=Grierson first2=Tim title=Every Judd Apatow Movie, Ranked From Worst to Best url=http://www.vulture.com/2015/07/every-apatow-movie-ranked-from-worst-to-best.html website=Vulture publisher=New York (magazine) New York accessdate=July 29, 2016 date=June 7, 2016}}</ref>

He tries to keep a low budget on his projects and usually makes his movies about the work itself rather than using big stars. After his success in film, he hired the entire writing staff from Undeclared to write movies for Apatow Productions.<ref name="latimes"/> He never fires writers and he keeps them on projects through all stages of productions, known colloquially as "the comedy wheel."<ref name="latimes"/> Apatow is not committed to any specific studio, but his projects are typically set up at Universal Pictures Universal and Sony Pictures Entertainment Sony,<ref name="latimes"/> and in 2009 Variety (magazine) Variety reported that Universal had signed him to a 3 picture directing deal.<ref>{{cite news last=Fleming first=Michael title=Universal embraces Judd Apatow work=Variety date=July 30, 2009 url=http://www.variety.com/article/VR1118006715?refCatId=13 accessdate=December 1, 2012}}</ref> Apatow once vowed to include a penis in every one of his movies.<ref>{{cite news last=Branney first=John title=Is Hollywood A No-Members Club work=The Guardian date=August 26, 2008 url=https://www.theguardian.com/film/filmblog/2008/aug/26/hasthefigleaffallen accessdate=April 23, 2009 quote=In an attempt to rid America of its phallophobia, Judd Apatow once vowed to include a penis in every one of his movies.  location=London}}</ref> He explained his position as, "I like movies that are, you know, uplifting and hopeful...and I like filth!"<ref>{{cite news last=Walker first=Tim title=King of bromance: Judd Apatow work=The Independent date=August 19, 2009 url=https://www.independent.co.uk/arts-entertainment/films/features/king-of-bromance-judd-apatow-1773842.html accessdate=November 14, 2010 quote=Says Apatow: "I like movies that are, you know, up-lifting and hopeful ... and I like filth." location=London, UK}}</ref>

===2009–2015: Funny People, This is 40, and Trainwreck===
Image:Funny People 6807.jpg thumb left Apatow (right) with Leslie Mann and Adam Sandler in Berlin (2009)
In 2009, Apatow served as producer for the biblical comedy film Year One (film) Year one; the film was not well received.<ref>{{cite web title=Year One (2009) url=https://www.rottentomatoes.com/m/year_one/ publisher=Rotten Tomatoes accessdate=July 29, 2016}}</ref> He also released his third directorial feature on July 31 that same year, titled Funny People. Apatow wrote the film, which starred Adam Sandler and Seth Rogen as a pair of standup comedians, one of whom has a terminal illness. Other co-stars included his wife Leslie Mann and Eric Bana, who was a comedic actor in Australia before appearing in American films. The film contained more dramatic elements than Apatows previous directorial efforts.<ref>{{cite web url=http://www.mtv.com/movies/news/articles/1588439/20080531/story.jhtml title=Adam Sandler To Star in Judd Apatow Movie About Stand-Up Comics date=May 31, 2008 first=Shawn last=Adler publisher=MTV accessdate=September 18, 2008}}</ref> Although the film was not financially successful (grossing $54 million domestically), the critical reception was primarily positive, with David Denby of New Yorker Magazine including the film on his top ten films of 2009,<ref>{{cite web last=Denby first=David title=The Ten Best Films of 2009 work=The New Yorker date=December 3, 2009 url=http://www.newyorker.com/online/blogs/tny/2009/12/denby-top-films-2009.html accessdate=December 1, 2012}}</ref> calling Funny People "a serious comedy about a funny mans brush with death" and stating that the film "is Apatows richest, most complicated movie yet—a summing up of his feelings about comedy and its relation to the rest of existence."<ref>{{cite web last=Denby first=David  title=Funny People Review work=The New Yorker date=August 3, 2009  url=http://www.newyorker.com/arts/critics/cinema/2009/08/03/090803crci_cinema_denby accessdate=December 1, 2012}}</ref> New York Times critic A.O. Scott, reflecting on summer films of 2009, opined that Funny People was "a movie about growing up, feeling sad, facing death—a long, serious film whose subject is the challenge of maturity. Which may be why, in the face of a softish opening weekend, various interpreters of box office data were quick to declare Funny People a flop. The summer is no time for grown-ups."<ref>{{cite news last=Scott first=A.O. title=Open Wide - Spoon-Fed at the Cinema work=The New York Times date=August 7, 2009 url=https://www.nytimes.com/2009/08/09/movies/09scot.html?pagewanted=all&_r=1& accessdate=December 1, 2012}}</ref>

In 2010, Apatow served as producer on the Forgetting Sarah Marshall spin-off Get Him to the Greek with Russell Brand reprising his role and Jonah Hill returning from the original film, albeit as a different character. Released by Universal, the film did well at the box office, grossing more than 60 million dollars.<ref>{{cite web title=Get Him to the Greek publisher=Box Office Mojo.com url=http://boxofficemojo.com/movies/?id=gethimtothegreek.htm accessdate=December 1, 2012}}</ref> In 2011, Apatow produced the most successful R-rated female comedy of all time, Bridesmaids (2011 film) Bridesmaids,<ref>{{cite web  title=Bridesmaids publisher=Box Office Mojo.com url=http://boxofficemojo.com/movies/?id=wiigapatow.htm accessdate=December 1, 2012}}</ref> which received Oscar Award Oscar nominations for Best Supporting Actress (Melissa McCarthy) and Best Original Screenplay (Annie Mumolo and Kristen Wiig), as well as two Golden Globe Award nominations and two Screen Actors Guild Award nominations. Bridesmaids also won a Critics Choice Movie Award for Best Comedy Movie, a Peoples Choice Award for Favorite Comedy Movie and was named one of AFIs Movies of the Year.<ref>{{cite web title=Bridesmaids work=Rotten Tomatoes url=http://www.rottentomatoes.com/m/bridesmaids_2011 accessdate=December 1, 2012}}</ref> That same year, he produced Girls (TV series) Girls after seeing Lena Dunhams 2010 film Tiny Furniture. In the 2013 Critics Choice Award Nominations from the Broadcast Film Critics Association, Apatows film This Is 40 was nominated for Best Comedy as were Leslie Mann and Paul Rudd for their performances in the film.<ref>{{cite web  last=Knegt  first=Peter  title="Lincoln" Leads 2013 Critics Choice Award Nominations  work=IndieWire  date=December 11, 2012  url=http://www.indiewire.com/article/2013-critics-choice-award-nominations-in-progress?page=1#articleHeaderPanel accessdate=January 7, 2013}}</ref> On January 10, 2013, the Broadcast Film Critics Association awarded Apatow the Critics Choice Louis XIII Genius Award named after a cognac.<ref>{{cite web last=Thompson first=Anne title=Judd Apatow To Receive Critics Choice Louis XIII Genius Award work=IndieWire date=December 20, 2012 url=http://blogs.indiewire.com/thompsononhollywood/judd-apatow-to-receive-critics-choice-louis-xiii-genius-award accessdate=January 7, 2013}}</ref> On October 3, 2013, The San Diego Film Festival awarded Apatow the esteemed Visionary Filmmaker Award.<ref>{{cite web last=Doniego de France first=Eileen  title=San Diego Film Festival Honors Hollywood Producer Judd Apatow work=La Jolla Blue Book date=October 11, 2013 url=http://www.lajollabluebook.com/blog/san-diego-film-festival-honors-hollywood-producer-judd-apatow accessdate=October 15, 2013}}</ref>

Apatow has been nominated for three Awards Circuit Community Awards, once for Best Director for the 2007 film Knocked Up and twice for Best Original Screenplay for Knocked Up and The 40-Year-Old Virgin, for which he shared the nomination with star and co-writer Steve Carell. Apatow was also nominated for the Writers Guild of America Award for Best Original Screenplay for his work on The 40-Year-Old Virgin, a nomination also shared with Carell, and Knocked Up.{{citation needed date=June 2018}}

In 2015, Apatows film Trainwreck (film) Trainwreck was nominated for 2 Golden Globe Awards, including a nomination for Golden Globe Award for Best Motion Picture – Musical or Comedy Best Motion Picture – Musical or Comedy, the first film directed by Apatow to achieve this feat.<ref>{{cite web title=Golden Globes 2016: Winners list url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/entertainment-arts-35060100 publisher=BBC News accessdate=July 29, 2016 date=January 11, 2016}}</ref> The film was also nominated for 3 Critics Choice Movie Awards, including Critics Choice Movie Award for Best Comedy Best Comedy.<ref>{{cite web last1=Rosen first1=Christopher title=Critics Choice Awards 2016 winners: Spotlight, Mad Max, Leonardo DiCaprio, and more url=http://www.ew.com/article/2016/01/17/critics-choice-awards-2016-winners-list website=Entertainment Weekly accessdate=July 29, 2016 date=January 17, 2016}}</ref>
