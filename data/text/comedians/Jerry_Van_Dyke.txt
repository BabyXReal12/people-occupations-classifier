Jerry McCord Van Dyke (July 27, 1931 – January 5, 2018) was an American actor, musician and comedian. He was the younger brother of Dick Van Dyke.<ref name=NYT>{{cite web title=Jerry Van Dyke url=https://www.nytimes.com/movies/person/72853/Jerry-Van-Dyke/biography work=The New York Times date=January 6, 2018}}</ref>

Van Dyke made his television acting debut on The Dick Van Dyke Show with several guest appearances as Rob Petries brother Stacey. While his infrequent starring roles were typically in poorly received sitcoms (My Mother the Car, one of the shows where he was the lead actor, is considered one of the List of sitcoms notable for negative reception worst sitcoms of all time), he enjoyed a long and successful career as a character actor in supporting and guest roles. From 1989 to 1997 he portrayed Luther Van Dam in Coach (TV series) Coach.<ref name=NYT />

==Early life==
Van Dyke was born in Danville, Illinois on July 27, 1931, to Hazel Victoria (Maiden and married names née McCord; 1896–1992), a stenographer, and Loren Wayne "Cookie" Van Dyke (1898–1976), a salesman.<ref name="Fox">{{cite web url=http://www.foxnews.com/entertainment/2018/01/06/jerry-van-dyke-comedian-and-actor-dead-at-age-86.html title=Jerry Van Dyke, comedian and actor, dead at age 86 publisher=Fox News Channel date=January 6, 2018 access-date=January 6, 2018}}</ref> He was of Dutch people Dutch, English people English, and Scottish people Scottish descent.<ref name="JVDB" /> His mother was a Mayflower descendant.<ref name="JVDB">{{cite web url=http://www.officialdickvandyke.com/biography/ title=Biography – The Official Site of Dick Van Dyke website=Officialdickvandyke.com access-date=January 6, 2018}}</ref>

==Career==
===Early career===

Following his first guest appearances on The Dick Van Dyke Show and two others on CBSs The Ed Sullivan Show, CBS made him a regular on The Judy Garland Show.<ref name="LAO">{{cite web url=http://www.latimes.com/entertainment/movies/la-et-st-jerry-van-dyke-obituary-20180106-story.html title=Jerry Van Dyke, comic actor known for TVs Coach, dies at 86 work=Los Angeles Times date=January 6, 2018 access-date=January 6, 2018}}</ref> He was also given hosting chores on the 1963 game show Picture This.<ref name="NYTobit" /> In that same year, movie audiences saw him in supporting roles in McLintock!, Palm Springs Weekend and The Courtship of Eddies Father (film) The Courtship of Eddies Father.<ref name="RSO" />

===Television career===
File:Jerry Van Dyke Accidental Family (cropped).JPG thumb right Van Dyke in a publicity photo for Accidental Family (1967)
In 1963 Van Dyke was cast on an episode of GE True, hosted by Jack Webb.<ref name="Variety">{{cite web url=https://variety.com/2018/tv/news/jerry-van-dyke-coach-actor-dead-dies-1202655598/ title=Jerry Van Dyke, Emmy-Nominated Coach Actor, Dies at 86 work=Variety date=January 6, 2018 access-date=January 6, 2018}}</ref> When The Judy Garland Show was unsuccessfully revamped, Van Dyke left the program.<ref name="TMZ" /> He turned down the offer to play Gilligan (Gilligans Island) Gilligan on Gilligans Island, a role which instead went to Bob Denver.<ref name="Variety" /> He rejected as well an offer to replace Don Knotts as Sheriff Andy Taylors deputy on The Andy Griffith Show.<ref name="Variety" /> Van Dyke finally accepted the lead role of attorney David Crabtree in My Mother the Car (1965), the misadventures of a man whose deceased mother Gladys (voiced by Ann Sothern) is reincarnated as a restored antique car.<ref name="NYTobit" /> Though the series was a commercial failure, Van Dyke continued to work steadily in supporting television and film roles through the rest of the decade.<ref name="NYTobit" /> He starred in another short-lived situation comedy Accidental Family (1967) as widowed comedian Jerry Webster who buys a farm to raise his son while he is not away on professional tours.<ref name="LAO" />

He was also featured in Love and Kisses (film) Love and Kisses (1965) and as Andy Griffiths co-star in Angel in My Pocket (1969).<ref name="Variety" />

During the 1970s Van Dyke returned to stand-up comedy. He spent much of the decade touring Playboy Clubs around the country and headlining venues in Las Vegas and Reno, Nevada, Summerfest in Milwaukee, and in Atlantic City, New Jersey.<ref name="Fox" /> He returned to television for guest appearances on Love, American Style and Fantasy Island.<ref name="Variety" /> In 1973 he portrayed Wes Callison, News Writer, on the season four episode "Son of But Seriously, Folks" on The Mary Tyler Moore Show.<ref name="EWO">{{cite web url=http://ew.com/tv/2018/01/06/jerry-van-dyke-dead-dick-van-dyke-brother/ title=Jerry Van Dyke, Coach Star and Dick Van Dykes Brother, Dies at 86 work=Entertainment Weekly date=January 6, 2018 access-date=January 6, 2018}}</ref> He also had roles in ABC Afterschool Special#Season Four .281975.E2.80.9376.29 The Amazing Cosmic Awareness of Duffy Moon (1976) and 13 Queens Boulevard (1979).<ref name="EWO" />Also in 1989 he appeared as a panelist in the pilot for the revival of Match Game, hosted by Bert Convy, but due to his brain tumor, he was later replaced by Ross Shafer when it was turned into a series in 1990.

In 1988 he made a guest appearance on Scott Baios sitcom Charles in Charge as Jamie Powells health teacher Mr. Merkin.<ref name="EWO" /> In 1989 Van Dyke began portraying beloved, yet befuddled, assistant coach Luther Van Dam on the long-running series Coach (TV series) Coach.<ref name="TMZ" /> For this role, he received four consecutive Emmy Award nominations (1990 through 1993) for "Outstanding Supporting Actor in a Comedy Series".<ref name="TMZ" />

==Personal life==
Van Dyke was married twice and had three children with first wife Carol, daughters Jerri Lynn and Kelly Jean and son Ronald.<ref name="NYTobit" /> Kelly Jean Van Dyke committed suicide in 1991, following struggles with substance abuse.<ref name=PM>{{cite web title=The Death of Twins Peak actor Jack Nance was as strange as the characters he played. url=http://www.people.com/people/archive/article/0,,20143430,00.html work=People (magazine) People access-date=March 13, 2014 author=Kyle Smith author2=Lorenzo Benet date=February 10, 1997}}</ref>

Jerry and wife Shirley resided together on their 800-acre ranch near Hot Springs, Arkansas.<ref name="TMZ" />

Van Dyke was an avid poker player and announced a number of poker tournaments for ESPN in the late 1990s and early 2000s.<ref name="Fox" /> He was also a four-string banjo player with several performances on The Dick Van Dyke Show to his credit.<ref name="JVDB" />

==Death==
On January 5, 2018, Van Dyke died at his Arkansas ranch, of heart failure at the age of 86.<ref name="NYTobit" /> He was in declining health since being involved in a car accident two years earlier.<ref name="TMZ">{{cite web  url=http://www.tmz.com/2018/01/06/jerry-van-dyke-dead-at-86/  title=Jerry Van Dyke Dead at 86  magazine=TMZ  date=January 6, 2018  access-date=January 6, 2018}}</ref>
