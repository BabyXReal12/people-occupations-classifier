Alan Young (born Angus Young; November 19, 1919&nbsp;– May 19, 2016) was a British Americans British&ndash;American actor, voice actor, comedian and radio and television host/personality who TV Guide called "The Charlie Chaplin of Television". He was best known for his role as naive Wilbur Post in the television comedy series Mister Ed (1961–1966). Young was also the voice of Disneys Scrooge McDuck for over thirty years, first in the Academy Award-nominated short film Mickeys Christmas Carol (1983) and in various other films, TV series and video games until his death. During the 1940s and 1950s, he starred in his own variety/comedy sketch shows The Alan Young Show on radio and television, the latter gaining him two Primetime Emmy Award Emmy Awards in 1951. He also appeared in a number of feature films, starting from 1946, including the 1960 film The Time Machine (1960 film) The Time Machine and from the 1980s gaining a new generation of viewers appearing in numerous Walt Disney Productions films as both an actor and voice actor.

==Early life==
Young was born as Angus Young on November 19, 1919, in North Shields, Northumberland, England, to Scottish people Scottish parents. (In his later years he claimed he had been born in 1924.) His father was a mine worker and a tap dancer, and his mother was a singer. The family moved to Edinburgh, Scotland, when Young was a toddler and to West Vancouver, British Columbia, Canada, when he was six years old. Young came to love radio when bedridden as a child because of severe asthma.<ref name=barnes>{{cite news  last1=Barnes  first1=Mike  last2=Byrnes  first2=Duane  date=May 20, 2016  title=Alan Young, Two-Legged Star of Mister Ed, Dies at 96  url=http://www.hollywoodreporter.com/news/alan-young-dead-mister-ed-816846  work=The Hollywood Reporter  location=  access-date=May 20, 2016 }}</ref><ref name=shapiro>{{cite news  last=Shapiro  first=T. Rees  date=May 20, 2016  title=Alan Young, Actor Who Played Willllburrrrr on ‘Mister Ed,’ Dies at 96  url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/entertainment/tv/alan-young-actor-who-played-wilbur-on-mister-ed-dies-at-96/2016/05/20/88ae8838-1ed4-11e6-8c7b-6931e66333e7_story.html  work=The Washington Post  location=  access-date=May 20, 2016 }}</ref>
By the time he was in high school, Young had his own comedy radio series on the Canadian Broadcasting Corporation CBC network, but he left it during the Second World War to serve in the Royal Canadian Navy.<ref name=barnes /><ref name=ap>{{cite news  last=Elbur  first=Lynn  date=May 20, 2016  title=Alan Young, Star of 1960s sitcom Mr. Ed, Dies at 96 url=http://bigstory.ap.org/article/7dc308dd967d4703aeffd12143b647b4/alan-young-star-1960s-sitcom-mr-ed-dies-96  work=Associated Press  location=  access-date=May 20, 2016 }}</ref><ref name="Stevens">{{cite book  last= Stevens  first= Christopher  title= Born Brilliant: The Life Of Kenneth Williams  publisher= John Murray  year= 2010  isbn = 1-84854-195-3  page=368}}</ref> He later resigned his Navy commission after learning he would be spending his time writing for a Navy show, and he attempted to join the Canadian Army. According to some sources, the Army rejected him due to his childhood asthma.<ref name=dunham>{{cite news  last=Dunham  first=Will  date=May 20, 2016  title=Actor Alan Young, Human Star of Horse Sitcom Mister Ed, Dies at 96  url=https://www.reuters.com/article/us-people-alanyoung-idUSKCN0YB2SJ  work=Reuters  location=  access-date=May 20, 2016 }}</ref>

==Career==
After leaving the service, Young moved to Toronto and resumed his Canadian radio career, where he was discovered by an American agent who brought him to New York City New York in 1944 to appear on American radio.<ref name=barnes /> Youngs first American radio appearances were on the Philco Radio Hall of Fame. This led to his own show, The Alan Young Show, National Broadcasting Company NBCs summer replacement for the series Eddie Cantor The Eddie Cantor Show.<ref name=barnes /> He switched to American Broadcasting Company ABC two years later, then returned to NBC.<ref>[https://books.google.com/books?id=EwtRbXNca0oC&pg=PA20 On the Air: The Encyclopedia of Old-Time Radio]  John Dunning; Oxford University Press, pp20&ndash;21</ref>

Youngs film debut was in Margie (film) Margie (1946), and he was featured in Chicken Every Sunday (1949).<ref>{{cite news last=Thomas first=Bob title=Comic Alan Young Critical of TV url=https://news.google.com/newspapers?id=WW1QAAAAIBAJ&sjid=VhAEAAAAIBAJ&pg=5217,4930447&dq=alan-young&hl=en newspaper=The Milwaukee Sentinel date=June 10, 1959}}</ref> In 1950, the television version of The Alan Young Show began. By 1951, the series received not only praise, but also several Primetime Emmy Awards, including Best Actor and Most Outstanding Personality for Young.<ref>{{cite web title=Nominations Search url=http://www.emmys.com/awards/nominations/award-search?search_api_views_fulltext=%22alan+young%22&submit=Search&field_celebrity_details_field_display_name=&field_show_details_field_nominee_show_nr_title=&field_show_details_field_network=All&field_show_details_field_production_company=All&field_nominations_year=1949-01-01+00%3A00%3A00&field_nominations_year_1=2016-01-01+00%3A00%3A00&field_award_category=All publisher=Academy of Television Arts & Sciences accessdate=25 May 2016}}</ref> After its cancellation, Young continued to act in films, among which Androcles and the Lion (film) Androcles and the Lion (1952) and Gentlemen Marry Brunettes (1955), and two George Pal films, Tom Thumb (film) Tom Thumb (1958) and The Time Machine (1960 film) The Time Machine (1960). He appeared in the NBC espionage drama Five Fingers (TV series) Five Fingers ("Thin Ice", 1959), starring David Hedison.<ref>[https://books.google.com/books?id=37SSBAAAQBAJ&printsec=frontcover  Theres No Business Like Show Business...Was]  Alan Young; BearManor Media, Jul 5, 2006, multiple mentions</ref>

Young was best known, however, for Mister Ed (1961–66), a Columbia Broadcasting System CBS television show, in which he starred as Wilbur Post, the owner of Mr. Ed, a talking horse who would not talk to anyone but him, thus causing comic situations for Wilbur Post, with his wife, neighbors, and acquaintances.<ref>{{cite news url=https://news.google.com/newspapers?nid=2202&dat=19610318&id=HD4mAAAAIBAJ&sjid=4v4FAAAAIBAJ&pg=2227,2840363 work=The Gettysburg Times date=March 18, 1961 title=Alan Young In Mister Ed accessdate=}}</ref> He also starred as Stanley Beamish in the unaired 1966 pilot episode of Mr. Terrific (TV series) Mr. Terrific, but apparently declined to appear in the broadcast series in 1967 that followed. In the late 1960s, he retired from acting for several years. During that time, he founded a broadcast division for the Christian Science Church.<ref>[https://books.google.com/books?id=4tcxAQAAIAAJ&q=%22alan+young%22+%22christian+science%22+broadcast&dq=%22alan+young%22+%22christian+science%22+broadcast&hl=en&sa=X&ved=0ahUKEwj7pZq5yvXMAhVGLlIKHSYICkwQ6AEIIjAB  TV Guide 1974]  page 36 "Well, hes come back to Hollywood after spending the past six years as a Christian Science lecturer in Boston and other parts of the country. "I set up a film and broadcasting department for the mother church in Boston," Young said"</ref>

During the 1970s, Young became active in voice acting. After 1974, he voiced Scrooge McDuck in numerous The Walt Disney Company Disney films and in the popular series DuckTales (1987-1990). In Mickeys Christmas Carol, he portrayed the characters miserly Ebenezer Scrooge namesake. He also played Scrooge in video games such as the Kingdom Hearts series, DuckTales: Remastered in 2013, and the Mickey Mouse (TV series) Mickey Mouse cartoon "Goofys First Love" released in 2015. Apart from Scrooge McDuck, his other prominent roles were Farmer Smurf on The Smurfs (TV series) The Smurfs, Haggis McHaggis on The Ren & Stimpy Show, 7-Zark-7, Keyop in Battle of the Planets, and Hiram Flaversham in The Great Mouse Detective. He also guest starred on The Love Boat, The Incredible Hulk (1982 TV series) The Incredible Hulk, The New Scooby and Scrappy-Doo Show, and Spider-Man and His Amazing Friends.<ref>{{cite web url=http://voicechasers.com/database/showprod.php?prodid=160 title=Spider-Man and His Amazing Friends (1981, Animated Series) Voice Cast website=Voice Chasers}}</ref>

Young later starred in the sitcom Coming of Age (1988 TV series) Coming of Age for two seasons from 1988-89. In 1991, Alan Young returned to the stage, starring as Capn Andy Hawkes in the California Musical Theatres adaptation of Show Boat. He had been called for the role after Van Johnson, who was initially cast in the part, was hospitalised.<ref>{{cite news author=Sylvie Drake url=http://articles.latimes.com/1991-09-16/entertainment/ca-1756_1_show-boat-stage date=September 16, 1991 title=Stage: Show Boat Afloat Without Its Star accessdate=}}</ref> He had also appeared in the plays A Funny Thing Happened on the Way to the Forum and The Girl With the Freudian Slip. In 1993, he recreated his role as Filby for the mini-sequel to George Pals The Time Machine, reuniting him with Rod Taylor, who had played George, the Time Traveller. It was called Time Machine: The Journey Back, directed by Clyde Lucas. In 1994, Young co-starred in the Eddie Murphy film Beverly Hills Cop III. He played the role of Uncle Dave Thornton, the Walt Disney-esque founder of the fictional California theme park Wonderworld, and in that same year, Young played the role of Charlie in the television film, Hart to Hart: Home Is Where the Hart Is.{{citation needed date=May 2016}}

After 1994, he played at least eight characters, including antique dealer Jack Allen on the radio drama Adventures in Odyssey. In 1997, he did the voice of Haggis McMutton in the PC game The Curse of Monkey Island. His later guest roles in animated series included Megas XLR, Static Shock, House of Mouse, The Ren & Stimpy Show, Duckman, Batman: The Animated Series and TaleSpin.<ref>[http://www.behindthevoiceactors.com/Alan-Young/ Alan Youngs voiceography]. Behind the Voice Actors.com – check mark indicates BTVA has verified the entries using screenshots of credits and other confirmed sources.</ref> In 2002, he had a cameo as the flower store worker in Simon Wells remake of The Time Machine (2002 film) The Time Machine, and in 2010, he read H.G. Wellss The Time Machine original novel for 7th Voyage Productions, Inc. Youngs television guest roles include Gibbsville (TV series) Gibbsville, The Love Boat, Murder, She Wrote, St. Elsewhere, Coach (TV series) Coach, Party of Five, The Wayans Bros., USA High, Hang Time (TV series) Hang Time, ER (TV series) ER, Maybe Its Me (TV series) Maybe Its Me, and Sabrina, the Teenage Witch (1996 TV series) Sabrina, the Teenage Witch ("Sweet Charity", 1997) in which he played Zeldas love interest.{{citation needed date=May 2016}}

==Personal life and death==
Young was married three times.<ref name=death>{{cite web url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/entertainment/tv/alan-young-actor-who-played-wilbur-on-mister-ed-dies-at-96/2016/05/20/88ae8838-1ed4-11e6-8c7b-6931e66333e7_story.html title=Alan Young, actor who played Willllburrrrr on ‘Mister Ed,’ dies at 96 author= T. Rees Shapiro  date=May 20, 2016 work=The Washington Post accessdate=May 20, 2016}}</ref> He and Mary Anne Grimes were married from 1941 to 1947 and had two children. He married Virginia McCurdy in 1948, and they had two children. They divorced in 1995, with Young marrying Mary Chipman the following year, but Young and Chipman then divorced in 1997.<ref name=amny>{{cite web url=http://www.amny.com/entertainment/celebrities/alan-young-dead-mister-ed-star-was-96-1.11823902 title=Alan Young dead; Mister Ed star was 96 author=Reuters publisher=AM New York date=May 20, 2016 accessdate=May 20, 2016 deadurl=yes archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20160522122307/http://www.amny.com/entertainment/celebrities/alan-young-dead-mister-ed-star-was-96-1.11823902 archivedate=May 22, 2016 df=mdy-all}}</ref> He was a Republican Party (United States) Republican.<ref>{{cite web url=https://books.google.com/books?id=QfHXAAAAQBAJ&pg=PA173&dq=Martha+Tilton+Republican&hl=en&sa=X&ved=0ahUKEwir-8ivuLDTAhUM8IMKHU_XAPUQ6AEILjAC#v=onepage&q=Alan+Young&f=false title=When Hollywood Was Right: How Movie Stars, Studio Moguls, and Big Business Remade American Politics first=Donald T. last=Critchlow date=October 21, 2013 publisher=Cambridge University Press via=Google Books}}</ref>

Young later lived in Woodland Hills, California, at the Motion Picture & Television Country House and Hospital, a retirement community, where he died of natural causes on May 19, 2016, at the age of 96.<ref name=death />
